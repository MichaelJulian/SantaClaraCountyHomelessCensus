Welcome to homestreet

Purpose: to aid homeless outreach organizations in their care for those living on the streets by providing analytics, insights, and an exploratory platform to better understand those living at our doorstep.

Author: Michael Julian - August 2016 - a capstone project for Galvanize's Data Science Immersive.

Learn more

Understanding the Data

Collection: The Point-in-Time Census is conducted every two years by surveying thousands of homeless across America. This analysis was completed on the Santa Clara County set, conducted by Applied Survey Research and obtained through the San Jose Homeless Outreach Team.

Content: The census contains information regarding Ethnicity, Sexuality, Reasons for homelessness, Prevention, Government Assistance, Household Information, and Health Conditions.

Composition: The data was cleaned from separate excel files and condensed to dummy variables that take the value of 1 or 0, depending on if the survey-taker answered "Yes."

Analyzing the Data

Predict High-Risk Condition

Model Requirements:

  • Strong Interpretability
  • Handle Imbalanced Classes
  • Favor False Positives over False Negatives

Reasoning: Homeless outreach teams and organizations that deliver aid may not have the resources to input data into a neural network that provides non-intuitive or black-box results. For this reason, Decision Tree Classifiers (strong interpretability) and Logistic Regression models (medium interpretability) will be utilized. Second, we should favor a model that favors false positives over false negatives because of the higher cost to society of a high-risk homeless going untreated.




Defining High-Risk Condition

A person will be defined as "high-risk" if they report:

  • Having a life-threatening health condition
  • Being a victim of domestic abuse
Imputing Data

Issue: Some respondents refuse to answer a question. This is a problem that becomes magnified in imbalanced classes and low sample sizes. Due to the wide nature (relative to respondents) of the dataset, synthetic minority oversampling would not provide trustworthy observations.

Use Cases Outside of Imputation: This is more than a simple imputation problem. There may be cases where an aid worker is dealing with a person who refuses to answer whether or not they are being abused. The aid worker can use other questions to see if this person is more similar to someone who answered "yes" to domestic abuse, than someone who answered "no."

Imputation Models

Possible Solutions:

  • Decision Tree model using other columns to predict imputed column
  • Use k-Nearest-Neighbors to find "most similar" respondents

Proposed Solution: Due to the high dimensionality of the data, I will first constrain the feature space to the top 3-5 features with the highest correlation to the target (domestic abuse). After this, the problem can be tackled through knn, naive bayes, or logistic regression.
